Project Details

The Energia Analyathon Challenge involved analyzing a dataset on the electricity industry across the All Island (AI), Northern Ireland (NI), and Republic of Ireland (IE), focusing on renewable energy sources such as wind, solar, and hydro energy. The goal was to identify similar trading days based on wind generation, energy demand, and net energy demand. The dataset was initially explored through various visualizations to understand its characteristics. Data preprocessing included calculating the net demand (difference between total demand and total generation), defining trading days, and ensuring data was segmented into 30-minute intervals for detailed analysis. Three approaches for data preparation were considered, with the final method involving the use of 30-minute aggregation to retain internal daily trends.

The similarity identification process employed the cosine similarity algorithm, which measures the angle between vectors to determine how closely the energy patterns of one day match another. This method provided an intuitive similarity score, allowing for easy comparison and ranking of similar days. To enhance accuracy, KMeans clustering was combined with cosine similarity, first clustering the data and then applying cosine similarity within clusters. This dual approach leveraged both distance-based metrics and vector similarity, yielding robust results. The project concluded with recommendations to integrate trading data insights for profitability-based modeling and to invest in energy storage technologies to mitigate the intermittency of renewable energy sources.

Greenspace Classification in Belfast

Deloitte's Analytathon 2 challenge focuses on classifying greenspaces within the Belfast area using Sentinel-2 L2A satellite images. The high-resolution images, covering 10-meter areas, include various green vegetation such as parks, forests, and street trees. The objective is to employ machine learning and deep learning algorithms to accurately label these greenspaces. The solution is divided into several parts: initially exploring the dataset by visualizing images and labels, creating a scene classification mask, splitting the dataset into training, testing, and validation sets, and finally, applying and evaluating various machine learning models to identify the most accurate greenspace classifier.

Project Details

For Deloitte's Analytathon 2 challenge, we classified greenspaces in Belfast using high-resolution Sentinel-2 L2A satellite images, aiming to accurately label green vegetation areas through machine learning and deep learning techniques. The dataset included True Colour, False Colour, and Scene Mask images. Preprocessing involved masking unwanted classes, splitting images into 256x256 pixels, and applying data augmentation (rotations, mirroring) to enhance variability. The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) was used to improve vegetation detection. The dataset was split into training (70%), testing (25%), and validation (5%) sets. We trained several models, including Bhavana_DeepLabV3, Maria_ResNet, U-Net, and U-Net+, with the U-Net+ model achieving the highest accuracy (99.82%) for greenspace classification.

Evaluation metrics such as ROC, AUC, Precision, and Recall validated the U-Net+ model's effectiveness. This model successfully identified and outlined greenspaces in Belfast. Future improvements include expanding the dataset, automating the data pipeline, and exploring multi-class segmentation. This project showcased the successful use of machine learning for environmental monitoring, providing insights into urban greenspaces. Key references included works on urban greenspace classification using Sentinel-2 imagery and machine learning techniques.

Project Details

The Analytathon Challenge aims to evaluate the financial impact of Hurricane Ian on properties insured by KDC Property Insurance. This involves calculating the maximum inherent cost of destruction for high-risk properties and identifying the most affected geographic areas. The process began with data preprocessing, which included cleansing the dataset, filling missing values using geolocation data, and addressing inconsistencies in the Sale Price and Market Price data by removing extreme outliers and imputing missing values. We then merged the property data with hurricane impact data through spatial joins, focusing on properties with more than 60% hurricane impact, specifically Single Family Residences and Condominiums. Financial exposure was determined by considering the higher of the Market Value or Sale Price and calculating Building, Contents, and Outbuilding Coverage to represent the total potential loss per property.

We identified the top five ZIP codes with the highest number of affected properties by filtering properties with more than 60% impact and analyzing the geographic spread of the damage. The results, visualized through an interactive dashboard, highlight the financial exposure and concentration of risk in these high-impact areas. The findings underscore the need for increased coverage in high-risk areas and proactive risk management measures to mitigate potential damages. Recommendations include adjusting insurance coverage limits and premiums in high-risk ZIP codes and engaging clients in these areas to implement comprehensive risk mitigation strategies.
